# Foundations of Systems Thinking, Lesson 1: A Different Way of Thinking

Lesson 1: A Different Way of Thinking. In this course, we'll explore systems thinking, a discipline that helps us see complexity more clearly and make better decisions as a result. In this first lesson, we'll explain what systems thinking is and contrast it with linear, cause-and-effect reasoning. Let's begin with an example. A customer service team is under pressure. Complaints are piling up and response times are slipping. The obvious solution seems clear: hire more staff. For a few weeks, things improve. Six months later however, the complaint backlog is back, and this time it's larger. So, what went wrong? The problem is linear thinking. Linear thinking follows a simple chain: problem leads to cause, cause leads to fix. It works well for problems that are genuinely simple and isolated, like a broken piece of equipment, or a single missing piece of data. However, most professional problems aren't like this. They involve multiple actors, competing pressures, and outcomes that take time to surface. When we apply linear thinking to those kinds of problems, we often get answers that are partially right in the short term but wrong over time, in ways we didn't anticipate. In our customer service example, adding staff reduced visible pressure, and that reduced pressure changed how leadership prioritised the underlying problems. With urgency removed, the root causes of complaints, that is: unclear processes and poor handoffs between teams, were never properly addressed. The system adapted around the fix, and the problem returned. So, how do we know when a problem is systemic rather than isolated? There are a few reliable signals. The first is that the same problem keeps returning, even after it appears to have been fixed. If a solution that worked last year isn't working now, the problem likely has structural roots that weren't addressed. The second signal is that different parts of the organisation experience the problem differently, and each blames the other. When every team has a reasonable account of what's going wrong and each account is at odds with the others, the issue is almost certainly in the interactions between those teams, not within any single one of them. The third is that well-intentioned interventions make things worse. If every fix creates a new problem, the system is pushing back against the intervention. That's a strong signal that the fix isn't addressing the right part of the system. Systemic problems are structural, and can't be resolved with simple fixes. To tackle systemic problems, we'll use 'systems thinking'. Systems thinking is a discipline that focuses on structures, relationships, and patterns of behaviour over time, rather than on individual events and single causes. 'Systems thinking' is an extension of 'analytical thinking'. Where analytical thinking asks "what happened?", systems thinking asks "what keeps producing this?" Where analytical thinking isolates a variable, systems thinking looks at the web of connections around it. It's worth being clear about what systems thinking is not. It isn't a method for analysing every possible variable in a situation. And it isn't about building elaborate diagrams before we're allowed to act. It's a practical discipline, and the vocabulary it uses is accessible to any professional willing to look at problems differently. In this course, we'll define what a system is and examine its structural building blocks: stocks, flows and feedback loops. We'll then explore the characteristic behaviours that complex systems produce including delays, emergence, and unintended consequences. Finally, we'll discuss how to apply a systems lens to real decisions at work through mental models, leverage points and informed action. By the end, we'll be able to identify systemic patterns, avoid common decision-making traps, and frame problems in ways that lead to more durable solutions. Let's stop the lesson here. In this lesson, we first examined why linear, cause-and-effect reasoning struggles when applied to complex problems. We then looked at the signals that tell us a problem is systemic rather than straightforward. Finally, we clarified what systems thinking is and what it isn't.
