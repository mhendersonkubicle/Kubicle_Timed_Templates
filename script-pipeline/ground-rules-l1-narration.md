# Lesson 1: Defining Team Ground Rules

Most project teams know what they need to deliver. Far fewer have agreed on how they will work together to deliver it. That gap, between task clarity and behavioral clarity, is where friction, confusion, and conflict tend to take root. In this course, we'll explore how team ground rules close that gap, and how to define, facilitate, document, and sustain them across a project's full lifecycle. In this first lesson, we'll distinguish team ground rules from general project policies and explain their role in enabling productive collaboration. Let's begin with a distinction that's easy to blur. A project policy is a formal rule or procedure: how change requests are submitted, how risks are escalated, how budgets are approved. These exist at the organizational or project-governance level, and they apply whether a team agrees with them or not. Ground rules are different. They are the behavioral norms a team sets for itself: how members communicate with one another, how disagreements are raised, how decisions get made when there's no single authority. They describe how the team will work together day to day, not just what the team is expected to produce. Ground rules are agreed behavioral norms that govern how a team works together, distinct from project policies or governance procedures. Ground rules are set by the team, for the team. Because members participate in creating them, they carry a sense of shared ownership that a policy document rarely achieves. It's tempting to treat ground rules as a soft formality, something that gets mentioned in a kick-off meeting and then forgotten. But that underestimates what they actually do. The first thing they create is psychological safety. When a team has agreed norms around how disagreement is handled, or how quieter voices get heard, people feel safer raising problems early. Research consistently shows that psychological safety, the belief that it's safe to speak up without penalty, is one of the strongest predictors of team effectiveness. Ground rules are a practical mechanism for building that safety into the team's structure rather than leaving it to chance. The second thing they do is reduce ambiguity. Without agreed norms, every interaction carries an implicit question: what are the rules here? Is it acceptable to challenge a senior colleague's estimate in a team meeting? How quickly is someone expected to respond to a message? These questions get answered one way or another, but without ground rules they're answered inconsistently, by whoever happens to have the most social authority in the moment. The third thing ground rules do is support accountability without requiring conflict. When a norm has been agreed collectively, calling it out when it's violated is not a personal criticism, it's a reference to a commitment the whole team made. That shift from personal to collective accountability changes how difficult conversations feel for everyone involved. Teams that skip ground rules don't avoid the need for norms. They just allow them to develop informally, which means they develop unevenly. Some members assume behaviors that others find frustrating. Dominant personalities set precedents that quieter members feel unable to challenge. Ambiguity about decision-making authority gets resolved through whoever escalates fastest, not through a shared understanding of the process. The pattern that tends to follow is a slow accumulation of small frustrations that eventually surface as larger conflicts. By the time the conflict is visible, it's carrying the weight of everything that wasn't said before. That's a much harder conversation to have than the one you could have had at the start. One last distinction worth making in this opening lesson: "ground rules" and "working agreements" are often used interchangeably, and for our purposes in this course that's fine. If there's a nuance, it's this: "ground rules" tends to emphasize the behavioral norms themselves, while "working agreement" tends to refer to the document that captures those norms in a format the team can refer back to. We'll use both terms throughout this course. When we talk about the process of facilitating and defining norms, we'll generally say "ground rules." When we talk about the artifact that records them, we'll say "working agreement.". In this course, we'll cover what ground rules are, where they fit in a team's development, and how to facilitate, document, and sustain them. By the end, we'll be able to design and lead a ground rule-setting process for a real project team and adapt it for hybrid or virtual contexts. In this lesson, we first distinguished team ground rules from project policies, establishing that ground rules are behavioral norms set by the team for the team. We then explored why they matter, covering their role in building psychological safety, reducing ambiguity, and enabling collective accountability. Finally, we introduced the distinction between ground rules as norms and working agreements as the documents that capture them.
